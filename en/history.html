History

Canadian Forces Base Borden History

A War is Looming

In 1914, an insurrection had begun in Sarejevo; the Canadian government realized its need to find a location to train soldiers was urgent. Steps were taken, and 18,000 acres, known as ‘The Sandy Plains’ were purchased at a cost of $1-3 per acre.

Building a Base

In April 1916, the 157th Battalion, now known as the Grey and Simcoe Foresters, from Barrie and Collingwood, arrived and began clearing the land, while living out of tents and washing in the Mad and Pine Rivers until four wash huts were built.

By July of 1916, Camp Borden was in full swing, resembling a tent city, with enough accommodations for 30,000 people at one time.

The War Years

Thousands of soldiers trained at CFB Borden, and development continued, including 18km of trenches built to simulate the trench warfare of WWI.

Two railway stations were established on Waterloo Road to help move soldiers in and out of the base. The rail lines were able to move 20,000 people a day.

Creating Space for the Air Force

As 1916 progressed, the United Kingdom requested space to train airmen. The Royal Flying Corps asked for a facility, and 1,000 acres of land was given to them at Camp Borden. The rail lines were then extended from the south end of the Base to the area where the airfield was to be built.

In 1917, men toiled in the snow and cold 7 days a week for three months: January, February and March, to construct 57 buildings to accommodate the 1,700 men that had been hired between Christmas and the New Year.

By the airfield’s official opening in May 1917, training was already in full swing. Amongst the first group of trainees was a young man named James Harold Talbot, who was Canada’s first military aviation fatality.

After the War

In 1924, Borden became the largest air station in Canada. During the 1930s, Borden was made a permanent Army training base. Signals, Armour, Infantry, Service Corps, Medical, Dental, Provost, Intelligence, and Nuclear, Biological and Chemical schools were all established in the years that followed.

In 1940, the Royal Canadian Air Force established what is now known as the Canadian Forces Ammunition Depot Angus. It also continued to train World War II pilots and aircrews.

Borden continued to be used as a flight centre, until the onset of the jet age, when the only training to be conducted here, with respect to the Air Force, was air maintenance.

The 1960s

In 1966, Borden became an integrated training base and the following schools were formed:

  • Canadian Forces School of Administration and Logistics (CFSAL)
  • Canadian Forces School of Aerospace and Ordnance Engineering (CFSAOE)
  • Canadian Forces Medical Service School (CFMSS)
  • Canadian Forces Dental Services School (CFDSS)
  • Canadian Forces School of Intelligence and Security (CFSIS)
  • Canadian Forces Nuclear, Biological and Chemical School (CFNBCS)
  • Canadian Forces School of Physical Education and Recreation (CFSPER)
  • Canadian Forces School of Instructional Technique (CFSIT)
  • Combat Arms School (CAS)

The 1970s & 80s

In 1970, the Armour and Infantry schools moved to Gagetown and the Language Training School was formed. The Canadian Forces School of Instructional Technique was renamed the Canadian Forces Training Development Centre (CFTDC).

The 1990s

In 1995, the Canadian Forces Recruiting, Education and Training System (CFRETS) moved from Trenton to Borden. The move was made to centralize headquarters staff functions.

In 1997, the Commander Training Schools (CTS) was formed, consolidating the Deputy Chief of Staff occupational training staff and the CFB Borden staff. In 1999, the CTS reorganized and became the Canadian Forces Support Training Group (CFSTG).

Today

In 2002, as part of the ongoing process of refining training, CFRETS was disbanded.

In 2013, with the restoration of the Canadian Army’s historical identities, the Canadian Forces School of Electrical and Mechanical Engineering (CFSEME) was renamed the Royal Canadian Electrical and Mechanical Engineers School (RCEME School).

Did you know?

  • An original wash hut from 1916 still exists today.
  • A WWI training trench has been restored and is available for public viewing.
  • Eight of the original Air Force buildings exist today.
  • The control tower at the new airfield, which still stands today, was named the Talbot Tower to honour the memory of James Harold Talbot. A memorial is housed in the RCAF museum annex at CFB Borden.

The Naming of CFB Borden

When CFB Borden opened in 1916, it was named after Sir Frederick Borden, who was the Minister of the Militia from 1896 to 1911.

While studying at the University of King’s College in Nova Scotia (NS), Borden joined the militia as a cadet. He later became an assistant surgeon in the 68th Battalion of Infantry in 1869 after receiving his MD from Harvard Medical School.

In 1874, Borden entered politics after making a name for himself through his various business ventures. By 1896, Borden was appointed as the Minister of Militia due to his militia experience, his membership in the parliamentary militia lobby, and his continued participation in the summer militia camp in Aldershot, NS.

Sir Frederick Borden’s Accomplishments

As Minister of Militia, Borden established rules regulating the tenure of command, decentralized command and administration, and extended Canadian military autonomy by reducing British control over the Canadian Forces with an amendment to the Militia Act in 1904.

Borden established schools of instruction, and reformed the Royal Military College of Canada, created a medical corps, an army service corps, an ordnance corps, a corps of engineers, a corps of guides, a corps of signals and a pay corps with increased pay and retirement benefits.

By 1901, Borden had been recognized for his work with Canada’s forces. The Broad Arrow, Britain’s military magazine, even stated, “No Minister of Militia has ever done so much toward making the Canadian Forces so thoroughly effective and ready for the field as he.”

It is for this reason, despite being out of office at the time, Camp Borden was named in his honour. Sir Frederick Borden died in January 1917, forever leaving his mark on the Canadian Armed Forces.
